Background

Motivation

I once worked in a smart home solution firm. I did learn some know-how of smart home. I decorated my home with HomeKit accessories.

After receiving structural and professional UI / UX training in 2021, and finished a team project on smart home app design. I designed the wireframe.

Side Project

After the course, I committed to exploring the smart home. I decided to focus on the platform, including iPadOS and macOS.

Apple Home is an integrated platform for smart home accessory manufacturers. Though it’s very convenient, but not that powerful because lots of accessories’ functions have been limited and canceled in Home.

User Story

First of all...

Apple Home is good, but some of my accessory’ function is missing.

To strengthen the functionality through expanding parameters for the accessory. The design will include...

  • Group the accessories and build a standard parameter list.
  • Authorize to visit the accessory’s native app, and save the setting.
And...

I would like to set up automation in several conditions, and it’s not working in Apple Home.

To expand the condition setting for automation through building logical and structural setting flow. The design will include...

  • Trigger - Condition - Scene flow.
  • Independent SCENE label.
However...

AI want a dashboard mode for my unused and wall-mounted iPad.

To provide dashboard mode for iPad through building large widgets and one-page UI.

  • Widgets ( time and environment status ) - Accessories layout on every page.
  • ROOMS label tab bars.
Finally...

It will be great if I can backup my settings or accessory’s code.

To provide backup function through code and setting backup. The design will include...

  • Sync with Apple ID.
  • Code export in the setting.

Goals

Idea

To keep the main function of Apple Home, but redesign the structure flow and UI for the Apple platform.

And a better CONTROL CENTER DASHBOARD, with large widgets, easy switches, and fewer pages.

Target user

This app is target to someone who...

  • New to Apple Home.
  • Looking for better experience and control than Apple Home.
  • Looking for a wall-mounted iPad that displays dashboard only without complex function.

Wireframs

Home page layout.

After a deep study of the competitors’ apps, I felt Apple Home’s home page layout was most suitable. For a tool application, the app’s function needed to be easy and convent to manipulate, and that’s why a block ( bigger ) is better than a list.

And to enhance the interaction experience. I put not the only status of the accessory, but with a menu icon so the user can quickly access to function page ( The user can tap the block to turn on or off the accessory for sure )

List view of accessories.

A block is easier to tap, but not to read, so what if the user wants to browse the accessories in the group? List view of accessories in iOS came to my mind.

I grouped the accessories with rooms, although accessories can also be displayed in the Room tab, sometimes, you may just forget the right room of the accessory you are looking for.

Structural design of control.

Structure means more than tidy. For front-end design, structural design forces the designer to focus on the foundation and major function of an accessory. For example, you may consider a light’s function so different from a camera, but they should both need a toggle to turn on or off, and the same structure to display some general manipulation such as rename and accessories’ info. And then you can design function pages respectively. And for back-end development, it reduces the time.

Dashboard view in iPad.

As mentioned in goals, some users mounted unused iPad on the wall to make it a dashboard. Dashboard represents a control center for a smart home. On some occasions dashboard is more convenient, for example, a too complicated instruction for your HomePod, or your iOS device is not available, or you just want to check the time, weather, or temperature.

Dashboard required some features, such as widgets that always display on the screen, a bigger block for an accessory, and a shortcut to the accessory’s function.

Style

Theme colors

To deliver a sense of deep and stability, I took ultramarine #1A56A2 in Hex, or ( 214, 84, 64 in HSB ) as the main color.

And to determine the secondary color, I kept the hue from main, and adjusted saturation to 10, brightness to 95, #DAE5F2 in Hex.

For auxiliary color, I kept the saturation and brightness from main, but minus 30 from hue to 194, #21A4CC in Hex.

Finally, for 3 scales of gray, I took from the Morandi Palette, which was #656667 for dark gray, #B4B4B4 for neutral gray, and #D4D1CD for light gray.

Icons

Filled icons are great, but it’s not easy to integrate colors, and it turns out to distract the main color. Then, smart home apps required more special icons than other apps, some of which are not suitable to fill. I chose a 2px stroke with main color #1A56A2 to build icons.

For join, to stay sharp, miter indeed.

In iOS, 22px icons are majors, 24px icons for tab bar. 24px icons takes the major place for 22px icons in iPadOS and macOS. 22px icons are majors for watchOS.

After lots of attempts, I designed the room’s icon with the most representative thing in the room, like a sofa for the living room.

Logo design

The logo design started with the image of a house, and the letter “m", “y” to stand for “my”.

The basic shapes are two overlapping horizontal flip corner cut rectangles, and for style consistency from icons, stroke was applied.

Finally to emphasize letters, main and auxiliary colors were used.

Rounded rectangle

Lists are the basic and most used components in this side project app. In order to make the app softer, rounded rectangles are widely used.

Lists are piled in groups, and the corner radius is set to 7 for the first and last child.

For other larger components like blocks and pop-up windows, the radius is set to 14.

Active and inactive

Filled and stroke may be the most seen method to distinguish icons in active or inactive, but as mentioned in icons, I decided to use light gray for inactive icons.
